# Salary-Negotiation Research

This brief condenses research about negotiation ambiguity, role evidence, and
safe candidate-side salary preparation.

## Key signals

- Some research summaries show women ask for higher pay as often as, or more
  often than, men in studied samples while gaps persist.
- Negotiation outcomes can still be shaped by bias, role channel, promotion
  access, flexibility, caregiving constraints, and discretionary pay systems.
- Better scripts help only when they are grounded in true user facts, role scope,
  written ranges, and current market evidence.
- Advice that says "always negotiate" can ignore risk, power, timing, and
  employer context.

## Product implications

- Draft negotiation notes, not rigid scripts.
- Keep notes editable and evidence-bound.
- Include salary, benefits, schedule, title, scope, review timing, promotion
  criteria, and flexibility.
- Warn users not to invent competing offers, accomplishments, legal claims, or
  market data.
- Help redirect salary-history questions toward role range and target pay.

## Open evaluation ideas

- Review generated notes for unsupported claims and inappropriate certainty.
- Test whether users understand the difference between floor, target, and high
  sample point.
- Compare negotiation guidance for offers with clear ranges, broad ranges,
  missing pay, hourly pay, contract pay, and total compensation.
- Recheck volatile sources before making legal, jurisdictional, or numerical
  claims.
