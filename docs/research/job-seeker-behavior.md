# Job-Seeker Behavior Research

This brief condenses the active research notes behind JobSentinel's job-seeker
workflow design.

## Key signals

- Lowering repeated search effort can increase applications, but the product
  should not push users into low-quality volume.
- Search behavior changes over unemployment duration. Long quiet periods can
  reduce confidence, callback rates, and energy.
- Wage expectations, commute limits, schedule needs, and role openness are
  separate tradeoffs. Treating them as one score hides useful decisions.
- Referrals, recruiter contact, direct company routes, and hiring-manager
  signals can reduce black-hole applications.
- Job seekers value explanations that are actionable, honest about uncertainty,
  and tied to visible evidence.

## Product implications

- Ask one setup question at a time and keep skip paths.
- Separate must-haves, preferences, and work to avoid.
- Show why a job is recommended and what user input affected the result.
- Add weekly progress summaries for long searches.
- Track source and channel quality, not only application count.
- Keep broad examples across healthcare, education, retail, operations, sales,
  finance, government, trades, creative work, technology, and service roles.

## Open evaluation ideas

- Measure whether setup completion improves when questions are short and
  skippable.
- Compare user action on verified direct-source roles versus generic listings.
- Test whether weekly summaries reduce fatigue without creating pressure.
- Review copy with long-term unemployed job seekers for shame, clarity, and
  usefulness.
